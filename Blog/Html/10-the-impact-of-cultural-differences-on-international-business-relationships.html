The Impact of Cultural Differences on International business relationships


As your enterprise grows to join the global economy, cultural sensitivity should be the next boardroom agenda if you wish to develop a competitive edge. From a business standpoint, culture refers to the customs, ideas, and the acceptable code of conduct that differs from one community to another.


Note that what may seem acceptable in one community or country may be a grave insult to the culture of another society. And actually, that is the whole point of cultural sensitivity. So, how exactly does culture affect the global marketplace, and why should you care?


Let’s deliberate on several key aspects.


Communication

Whether local or international environments, how you communicate with your prospects can be the difference between thriving and surviving. However, communication in the global economy goes beyond the usual language barriers. It is more of how you convey your message, the tone, mannerisms, and the gestures you use.


All that zeroes in to the general behavior that different communities expect. For instance, in a US or Germany ecosystem, it is okay to be loud and assertive when talking to your prospects. But in a country like Japan, being soft and passive will help you close even more deals.


Therefore, using the appropriate language and being sensitive to both verbal and non-verbal communication can steer your business to tremendous success.


Organizational hierarchy

Different cultures look at ranks from different perspectives. For example, consider Sweden—a Scavandian country and, once again, Japan.


Sweden people value equality and tend to disregard their ranks at the workplace—they embrace a flat organizational hierarchy. However, the Japanese tradition expects the exact opposite. Those in senior positions deserve absolute respect and regard from their juniors.


So, this should even translate to the way you assign roles in your global business.


Workplace etiquette

Etiquette ranges from the formality of addressing your business partners and clients to working hours and your adherence to rules and regulations.


When it comes to addressing people, different communities prefer either the first name or last name. If you are dealing with the Chinese, consider using the latter. But for Americans, their first name will be quite okay.


Also, some communities consider working for extra hours as a demonstration of hard work and commitment. However, in other cultures, this may be interpreted as a lack of concern for personal and family time.


Laws

Cultures have different interpretations when it comes to adherence to business laws. In some countries, running an unregistered or illegal business may not only attract a legal penalty but also, it may send away your clients. So, learning and adhering to the laws is quite imperative.


Don’t lose your international deals

Be keen not to lose valuable international deals because you ignored these aspects. Before venturing into the global market, consider doing in-depth research to establish the culture of your target audience. Think about their language, communication mannerisms, how they interpret formality in business, and the laws governing business operations.
